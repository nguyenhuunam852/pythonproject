article-body"><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">According to the Oxford Dictionary (and anyone who has ever studied English as a second language), spelling is hard. And <a href="https://www.inc.com/melanie-curtin/7-common-spelling-mistakes-that-can-make-you-look-.html">misspellings</a> are not only common, but also awkward in <a href="https://www.inc.com/peter-economy/17-spelling-errors-that-make-you-look-really-unprofessional.html">professional</a> contexts.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">When you receive an email or document with spelling errors, it's difficult to trust the person sending it. Spelling speaks to attention to detail as well as level of education, and you want to make sure yours is on point.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Here are the top 10 most misspelled words in the English language, according to the <a target="_blank" rel="nofollow" href="http://blog.oxforddictionaries.com/2016/08/02/corpus-misspellings/">Oxford Dictionary</a>:</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">10. Accomodate​</h2></div><div id="native_mid_article_inject"><div class="adElement AdContainer__adContainer__3lfQm " id="native_mid_article_inject"><div style="width:8px;height:1px"></div></div></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">This is one that often shows up in business communications, so you want to make sure you get it right. It actually takes&nbsp;two <em>c</em>'s and two <em>m</em>'s.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: accommodate</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">9. Wich​</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">It seems hard to believe that you could put down "wich" when you really meant "which" -- but this one has more than&nbsp;two million mentions in the Oxford Dictionary's corpus,&nbsp;more mentions than any other word on this list.</p></div><div class="d-md-none mobile_insert_9"></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: which</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">8. Recieve​</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Despite the fact that this is drilled in&nbsp;elementary school and beyond, it's apparently still challenging to remember that the rule is ... (all together now)&nbsp;"<em>i</em> before <em>e</em>, except after <em>c</em>."&nbsp;This is one of the <em>c</em>'s in question.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: receive</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">7. Untill​</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">This was originally the correct spelling of the word ... in the Middle Ages. We've (arguably) advanced since that period of human history, and the word only has one "l<em>"</em> these days. You can also get away with the abbreviation 'til, if you're feeling bold (it's still grammatically correct).</p></div><div class="d-md-none mobile_insert_15"></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: until</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">6. Occured</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p>Similar to <em>accommodate</em>, you've got double consonants in this one. It might also <em>occur</em> to you that there's only one<em> r</em>&nbsp;in the word when it's in present tense, which makes it extra confusing.&nbsp;Welcome to English.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: occurred</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">5. Seperate</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p>Again, you want to get this right in business settings, such as&nbsp;when telling your team to "send separate emails" to different clients.</p></div><div class="d-md-none mobile_insert_21"></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: separate</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">4. Goverment​</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">You've got to <em>govern</em> in order to have a government. Don't forget about that <em>n</em> in there.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: government</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">3. Definately​</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">This is definitely one you want to get right. If you just remember, "I want to get <em>it</em> right," then you can remember that there's an <em>i </em>in the middle there, not an <em>a.</em></p></div><div class="d-md-none mobile_insert_27"></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: definitely</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">2. Pharoah</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Probably not one that'll come up a lot at work (unless you've got quite the totalitarian C-suite), but still good to know. Also&nbsp;an idea for a&nbsp;Halloween costume.</p></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Correct spelling: pharaoh</p></div><div class="standardText"><h2 dir="ltr">1. Publically</h2></div><div class="standardText"><p dir="ltr">Whether you use it privately or publicly, this is one you want to make sure is correct.</p></div><div class="standardText lastItem"><p
